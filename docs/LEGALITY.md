# Legality — cooling without breaking the law

Depaving is not always legal. Most sealed squares on the board are
land the player cannot lawfully touch: roads, sidewalks, schoolyards,
other people's property. A game that celebrates flipping squares must
therefore teach the legal path to every act, or it is inciting
criminal damage at continental scale. This file is the education the
product ships; the binding design rule is rule 8 in GAME_DESIGN.md.

Nothing here is legal advice. Rules are municipal and they change;
the player's city hall is the authority. The product's promise is
narrower and absolute: **the game never asks for an illegal act.**
Every act pitch names a lawful route, and where none exists for this
player on this square, the lawful route is the petition.

## The tenure ladder

Who controls the ground decides what you may do to it. Three rungs:

### 1. Your own ground — garden, courtyard, your own roof

Mostly yours to change, with caveats the panel must name:

- **Utilities first.** Pipes and cables run under private paving
  too. Every EU country runs a call-before-you-dig service (KLIC in
  NL, DICT in FR, Leitungsauskunft in DE, ...). Free — and asking
  before digging is sometimes mandatory, not just wise.
- **Heritage and façade rules** can restrict visible changes: cool
  (light) paint on a protected façade may need a permit.
- **Drainage rules** usually *favor* depaving — many cities cap or
  tax sealed surface — but runoff toward neighbours is regulated.
- **Co-ownership**: a shared courtyard needs the owners'
  association's yes, in writing.

### 2. Rented ground

The tenant dwells, the owner decides. Get written consent — many
landlords say yes to a greener yard, and some municipal subsidy
schemes require that they be asked. Meanwhile a planter or pot needs
nobody's permission and still shades and cools.

### 3. Public and institutional ground — most of the board

Never DIY. Prying a public tile without permission is criminal
damage in most jurisdictions, however green the motive. The lawful
levers, roughly in order of effort:

- **Façade-garden schemes** (NL "geveltuin"): many cities let
  residents green the sidewalk strip against their own façade,
  permit-free within stated depth and utility rules. The closest
  legal cousin of guerrilla depaving.
- **Tree-pit adoption**: cities hand residents the planting and
  care of existing street tree pits. New pits are the city's to cut.
- **Official depave programs**: NK Tegelwippen's tile collection,
  municipal depave subsidies and depave weeks — the city lifts the
  tiles because you asked.
- **Grants for institutions**: schoolyard and courtyard greening
  funds exist across the EU; the school signs, not you.
- **Participatory budgets and citizen initiatives**: formal channels
  for proposing a specific square to the council.
- **The block petition** (in-game): a standing, visible, revocable
  signature local governance can see. Always legal, everywhere.

## Per act

- **Depave** — own garden: usually free after the utilities check;
  rented/shared: written consent; public: program or petition only.
- **Tree pit** — own ground: free, minding species and distance
  rules near boundaries; public sidewalk: adoption schemes only —
  cutting new holes in public pavement is always the city's call.
- **Cool surface** — your own roof: usually free; a façade may hit
  heritage rules; someone else's building: the owner's decision —
  petition the owner, not the paint bucket.

## Always legal, everywhere

Joining the block petition. Planters and pots. Watering a street
tree. Shading your own windows. Writing to your council. Recruiting
a neighbour. The game keeps at least one of these one click from
every square: no square is a dead end where the only visible move
is unlawful.

## Where the product teaches this

- **The square panel**: the pledge buttons sit under a "legal path"
  explainer keyed to the tenure ladder (web PixelPanel).
- **The steps** under the header split "yours to change" from "not
  yours to touch" and route the latter to the petition.
- **Design rule 8** (GAME_DESIGN.md): the game never asks for an
  illegal act; every act pitch names its lawful route.
